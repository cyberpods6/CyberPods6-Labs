# Forensic Science & Investigation Methodology
**Computer forensics** is the process of identifying, collecting, preserving, analyzing, and reporting on digital evidence to understand incidents and support legal proceedings. Forensic readiness differs from traditional incident response by proactively preparing an organization to efficiently collect and preserve digital evidence before an incident occurs, reducing costs and seamlessly integrating with business operations.

### The Investigation Process
   
   Common methodologies include the SANS Investigative Forensic Toolkit (SIFT), NIST SP 800-86, and the Digital Forensics Research Workshop (DFRWS) model. The general phases include:

   1. **First Response**: Securing and documenting the initial scene to prevent evidence contamination.

   2. **Evidence Preservation**: Using write-blockers, hashing, and maintaining a strict chain of custody.

   3. **Data Acquisition**: Collecting volatile and non-volatile data following the order of volatility.

   4. **Data Analysis**: Interpreting data to uncover artifacts and indicators of compromise (IoC).

   5. **Reporting & Testimony**: Documenting methodologies and findings, and providing expert, objective testimony in court.

### Ethics, Laws, and Search & Seizure
Digital forensic investigators must remain objective, impartial, and maintain the highest standards of integrity. Enhancing or altering evidence to support a case is highly unethical.

- **The Fourth Amendment**: Protects against unreasonable searches and requires a warrant to be supported by probable cause, specifically detailing the items to be seized (particularity requirement).

- **Plain View Doctrine**: Allows investigators to seize evidence of a crime that is immediately visible during a legitimate, lawful search of a computer.

- **Rule 41(e)(2)(B)**: Allows a two-step process for digital evidence: an initial broad seizure of electronic storage media followed by a thorough, off-site forensic search restricted to the scope of the warrant.
