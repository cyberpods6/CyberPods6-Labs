## Module 01: Computer Forensics in Today’s World

### Understand the Fundamentals of Computer Forensics
Computer forensics involves identifying, collecting, preserving, analyzing, and reporting on digital evidence to be used in court. A foundational concept is **Locard's Exchange Principle**, which states that anyone entering a crime scene leaves something behind and takes something away. Investigations fall into three categories: **Criminal** (proof beyond a reasonable doubt), **Civil** (preponderance of truth, often involving disputes/damages), and **Administrative** (internal corporate probes).

### Understand Cybercrimes and their Investigation Procedures
**Cybercrimes** use computers or networks as tools, targets, or storage for illicit activities. Common threats include **phishing**, **identity theft**, **ransomware**, **Business Email Compromise (BEC)**, **Denial of Service (DoS)**, and **cyber espionage**. Investigation procedures dictate a strict methodology to safely gather volatile and non-volatile evidence without disrupting operations or corrupting the evidence.

### Understand Digital Evidence and eDiscovery
**Digital evidence** is any data stored or transmitted digitally that can support a legal theory. **eDiscovery** is the official process of identifying, collecting, and searching Electronically Stored Information (ESI) for litigation. This follows the **Electronic Discovery Reference Model (EDRM)** and relies heavily on issuing a **litigation hold** to halt normal data deletion. Failing to preserve data leads to **spoliation of evidence**, which can ruin a legal case.

### Understand Forensic Readiness
**Forensic readiness** is a proactive approach where organizations implement logging, technical controls, and policies before an incident occurs. Unlike reactive incident response, forensic readiness minimizes investigation costs and ensures digital evidence is systematically preserved to integrate seamlessly with business operations.

### Understand the Role of Various Processes and Technologies
Digital forensics works alongside **vulnerability assessments** and **Network Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)**. Investigators rely on centralized logging and **SIEM** platforms to aggregate text-based and event logs to construct an accurate timeline of an intrusion.

### Identify the Roles and Responsibilities of a Forensic Investigator
Investigators act as **first responders**, analysts, and **expert witnesses**. As an expert witness, the investigator must translate complex technical findings to the court while maintaining strict neutrality and avoiding unexplained technical jargon. They operate under a **Code of Ethics** prioritizing objectivity, impartiality, integrity, and strict legal compliance. Enhancing or altering evidence to support a case is highly unethical.

### Understand the Challenges Faced in Investigating Cybercrimes
Investigators face massive hurdles including data volatility, **encryption**, **anti-forensics techniques**, **steganography**, jurisdictional issues, and diverse media formats. Identifying these challenges early allows investigators to prioritize the collection of live, volatile data (RAM) before it vanishes.

### Understand Various Standards and Best Practices
Compliance with standards ensures repeatable, scientifically sound investigations. Key organizations include **ISO/IEC (27037 series)**, **SWGDE** (Scientific Working Group on Digital Evidence), **NIST**, and **ENFSI**. Following these best practices prevents defense attorneys from successfully arguing that evidence was improperly handled (spoliation).

### Understand Laws and Legal Compliance
Investigators must adhere to laws governing search and seizure.
*   **The Fourth Amendment:** Protects against unreasonable searches and requires a warrant to be supported by probable cause. It includes the **particularity requirement**, which mandates that warrants specifically detail what may be searched and seized.
*   **Plain View Doctrine:** Permits the seizure of evidence of a crime that is immediately visible during a lawful search.
*   **Rule 41(e)(2)(B):** Allows a two-step process: an initial broad seizure of electronic storage media followed by a thorough, off-site forensic search restricted to the scope of the warrant.
