# forensics-federal-rules-of-evidence
The **Federal Rules of Evidence** are a comprehensive set of rules that govern the introduction and use of evidence in civil and criminal trials in United States federal courts. These rules were initially enacted by Congress in 1975 after being drafted by the Supreme Court. They are designed to ensure that legal proceedings are conducted fairly, efficiently, and with a focus on ascertaining the truth.

## Notable Rules of Evidence
**Rule 103: Rulings on Evidence**
- **Description**: Rule 103 of the Federal Rules of Evidence addresses how errors in rulings on evidence can be claimed. A party can only claim an error if it affects a substantial right and if the error was properly brought to the court's attention. This includes making timely objections or motions to strike and stating specific grounds for the objection unless the context makes it apparent. The rule also allows for the preservation of claims of error for appeal without needing to renew objections once a definitive ruling is made on the record[1][2][5].

- **Use Case**: During a trial, a party objects to the admission of a piece of evidence, arguing that it violates the rules of evidence. The objection is noted on the record, and the judge makes a ruling. The party does not need to object again to preserve the issue for appeal if the ruling is definitive[1][5].

**Rule 105: Limited Admissibility**
- **Description**: Rule 105 deals with situations where evidence is admissible for one purpose but not for another. It allows the court to restrict the evidence to its proper scope and instruct the jury accordingly to prevent misuse of the evidence. This ensures that evidence is only considered for its intended purpose[5].

- **Use Case**: In a criminal trial, a defendant's prior conviction is admissible only to assess credibility, not to suggest a propensity to commit the crime charged. The judge instructs the jury to consider the evidence solely for credibility assessment[5].

**Rule 502: Attorney-Client Privilege**
- **Description**: Rule 502 addresses the attorney-client privilege and the work-product doctrine. It aims to limit the waiver of these privileges, particularly in federal proceedings. The rule provides that disclosure of privileged information does not constitute a waiver if it was inadvertent and reasonable steps were taken to prevent and rectify the error[5].

- **Use Case**: During discovery, a party accidentally discloses a document containing privileged attorney-client communications. The party promptly takes steps to rectify the error, and under Rule 502, the privilege is not waived because the disclosure was inadvertent and reasonable measures were taken to prevent it[5].
