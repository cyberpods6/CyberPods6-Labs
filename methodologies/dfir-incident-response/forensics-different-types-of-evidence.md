# forensics_different_types_of_eveidence
Several types of evidence can be collected and analyzed in a forensics investigation:
### Testimonial Evidence
- **Oral Evidence**: Statements or spoken testimony from eyewitnesses, victims, or suspects regarding the incident.[1][4] This includes oral evidence presented by eyewitnesses to the incident.
### Physical/Real Evidence
- **Biological Evidence**: Blood, semen, saliva, hair, etc. that can be analyzed for DNA.[1][4]
- **Trace Evidence**: Fibers, soil, glass fragments, paint chips, gunshot residue, etc. that can link a suspect to a crime scene.[1][4]  
- **Impression Evidence**: Footprints, tire tracks, tool marks, etc. that can be cast or photographed.[1][4]
- **Fingerprint Evidence**: Latent fingerprints lifted from surfaces at the crime scene.[1][4]
- **Ballistic Evidence**: Bullets, cartridge casings, gunshot residue patterns, etc.[1]
- **Arson Evidence**: Accelerants, burn patterns, debris analysis in cases of suspected arson.[1]
- **Digital Evidence**: Data from computers, phones, cameras, etc. that may contain relevant information.[1]
**Documentary Evidence**
- **Written Documents**: Contracts, letters, notes, receipts, etc. that are relevant to the case.[1][2]
- **Photographic/Video Evidence**: Photographs, CCTV footage, or other recordings related to the incident.[3]

The different types of evidence are subject to specific rules and procedures for collection, analysis, and presentation in court to ensure their admissibility and credibility.[3] Combining multiple forms of evidence can help investigators reconstruct the events and circumstances surrounding a crime.

## Primary vs Secondary Evidence
- **Primary evidence** refers to original, firsthand sources of information or objects directly related to an incident or case. 
- **Secondary evidence** refers to information derived from primary sources, such as copies, summaries, or testimonies about the primary evidence.

Some examples of primary and secondary evidence in a forensics investigation include:

### Primary Evidence
- Biological evidence (blood, semen, hair, etc.) collected from the crime scene
- Fingerprints lifted from surfaces at the crime scene
- Original documents or notes related to the case
- Photographs or video footage of the crime scene or incident
- The murder weapon or other physical objects used in the crime
- Eyewitness testimony from individuals present during the incident[1]

### Secondary Evidence
- Copies or photocopies of original documents related to the case
- Testimony from individuals not present at the incident, recounting what they heard from others[1]
- Summaries or transcripts of eyewitness accounts or testimonies
- Recreations or animations depicting the crime based on evidence
- Expert analysis or opinions based on examination of primary evidence[1]

It's important to note that primary evidence is generally considered more reliable and carries more weight in court than secondary evidence. However, secondary evidence can still be valuable in corroborating or providing context for primary evidence when the original sources are unavailable or disputed.[1]

## Direct evidence and indirect (circumstantial) evidence
Direct evidence and indirect (circumstantial) evidence are two distinct types of evidence that can be presented in a forensics investigation or legal case. Here are the key differences between them:

## Direct Evidence
- Directly proves a fact in question without needing any inferences or assumptions to be made[1][2][3][4][5]
- Examples: Eyewitness testimony of directly observing an event, video footage capturing the incident, the defendant's fingerprints on the murder weapon, a written confession[1][2][4][5]
- Considered more reliable and impactful as it directly establishes the fact[2]

## Indirect/Circumstantial Evidence
- Does not directly prove the fact in question, but provides evidence of other facts from which one can infer or conclude the fact in question[1][2][3][4][5]  
- Examples: Bloodstains, gunshot residue, witness seeing the defendant fleeing the crime scene, internet search history related to the crime[1][2][4][5]
- Requires drawing reasonable inferences and conclusions from the circumstantial evidence presented[3][4][5]

The weight and credibility given to each type of evidence ultimately depends on the specific facts and circumstances of the case, as well as the discretion of the judge or jury[2][3].
