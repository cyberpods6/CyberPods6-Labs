# forensics-warrants
## Do not need a search warrant when
Police officers do not need a search warrant in the following situations:

- **Consent**: If the person in control of the premises voluntarily gives consent to search, no warrant is required.[1][2] However, the consent must be freely given without coercion.

- **Plain view doctrine**: If officers are lawfully present and see evidence of a crime in plain view, they can seize it without a warrant.[1][2][4] 

- **Hot pursuit**: Officers can enter private premises without a warrant if they are in hot pursuit of a fleeing suspect.[1][4] They can then search for the suspect and any related evidence.

- **Exigent circumstances**: A warrantless search is permitted if officers reasonably believe that evidence could be destroyed or someone's safety is at risk if they delay to get a warrant.[1][2][4] For example, hearing gunshots from inside a home.

- **Search incident to arrest**: After a lawful arrest, officers can search the arrestee and their immediate surroundings without a warrant to prevent destruction of evidence or protect officer safety.[2][4][5]

- **Automobile exception**: Officers can search a vehicle without a warrant if they have probable cause to believe it contains evidence of a crime.[4]

## Consent Searches
The key differences between consent searches and other warrantless searches are:

- Consent searches require voluntary permission from the person whose property is being searched. Other warrantless searches like plain view, exigent circumstances, search incident to arrest, etc. do not require consent.

- For a consent search to be valid, the person giving consent must have authority over the premises being searched. For other warrantless searches, authority is not required as long as the legal exception applies.[1]

- Consent can be limited in scope (e.g. consenting to search a car but not the trunk). Other warrantless searches allow officers to search areas permitted by the specific exception.[4]

- Consent can be revoked at any time during the search, terminating the officer's authority to continue searching. This is not the case for other warrantless searches where the legal exception still applies.[1]

So in summary, a consent search derives its legal justification from the voluntary permission of the person in control of the property. Other warrantless searches are legally justified by specific exceptions to the warrant requirement outlined in case law, without needing consent.[2][3][5]
