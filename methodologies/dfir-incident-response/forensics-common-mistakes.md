# forensics-common-mistakes
## Some common mistakes made by first responders and forensic practitioners
Here are some common mistakes made by first responders and forensic practitioners in handling forensic evidence:

### Forensic Science Report Errors
- Misstatements or errors in forensic science reports due to lab errors, poor communication, or resource constraints.[1]
- Failure to collect reference samples or conduct forensic tests correctly, such as in serological (blood) typing cases.[1]

### Evidence Handling Mistakes
- Failure to properly collect, examine, or report potentially probative forensic evidence during investigations.[1]
- Compromising the chain of custody or losing evidence.[1]
- Police misconduct related to evidence handling.[1]

### Testimony Errors
- Mischaracterizing statistical weight, probabilities, or limitations of forensic analysis during testimony.[1][4]
- Overstating the conclusions that can be drawn from forensic disciplines lacking a strong scientific foundation like bite mark analysis, hair comparisons, or arson investigation.[4]

### Cognitive Biases
- Confirmation bias, where analysts focus only on evidence supporting their initial theory and discount contradictory evidence.[5]
- Contextual bias, where irrelevant background information about a case influences the objective examination of evidence.[5]

### Other Common Mistakes
- Using forensic techniques or offering testimony that does not meet current scientific standards and guidelines.[1][4] 
- Lack of continuous training to keep up with rapidly evolving forensic methods and technologies.[3]
- Over-reliance on a single forensic tool instead of cross-checking results with multiple tools.[3]
- Incompetent, undertrained, or fraudulent forensic examiners.[1][2]

To minimize mistakes, it's crucial for first responders and forensic practitioners to follow proper protocols, receive ongoing training, avoid cognitive biases, and maintain scientific objectivity when handling evidence.[1][3][4][5]
